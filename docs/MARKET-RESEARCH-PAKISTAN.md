## 1. THE MARKET — layer by layer

**Layer 1 — the behaviour is already national.**
- ~**41% of Pakistanis** have participated in a committee/kameti; roughly **US$5 billion a year** rotates through them (Oraan research, via TechCrunch 2021). No education cost, no habit creation — the product's job is removing flaws from behaviour that already exists at national scale.
- Committees are heavily **women's finance**: organised by women, for household goals (Oraan's user base is 84% women). The population formal finance serves worst runs the informal instrument that performs best.
- The dark side that regulators and journalists know by name: the **Karachi Rs 400M online-committee fraud** — a single operator holding everyone's money. Every committee scam shares that root; it is precisely the thing our record-only architecture makes impossible. The scam headlines are, perversely, our best marketing context: everyone knows the problem is real.

**Layer 2 — the exclusion that sustains it.**
- Pakistan is one of just **8 countries holding ~650M of the world's 1.3B unbanked** adults (World Bank **Findex** 2025); account ownership remains a minority of adults, with a ~**30-point gender gap** and only **24% of women owning a phone vs 89% of men**.
- Practical consequence: the underserved majority is reachable through **hosts and groups** (assisted, Urdu-first, trust-borne), not through solo app downloads. Our growth unit — the host who brings her existing circle — is not a tactic, it's the only distribution that works down-market (the wallet era proved features don't beat trusted distribution; SimSim was free, first, and lost).

**Layer 3 — the adjacent formal market (our partners, not competitors).**
- Microfinance (**PMN MicroWatch**, Dec-2024): **12.34M active borrowers** — ~72% of *all* borrowers in the financial system — Rs **604B** gross loans (average ~Rs 49,000 — almost exactly one committee payout), Rs **733B** deposits, 4,145 branches in 139 districts. Enormous reach, thin wallet share: the segment saves *around* banks, not in them.
- Banking sector deposits ~Rs **30+ trillion**, with **Islamic banking** around a fifth of the system and growing under the Federal Shariat Court's directive to eliminate riba (conversion horizon ~2027) — a regulatory tailwind for *anything* structurally halal.
- **SBP's digital-bank licences** (Easypaisa Bank, Hugo, KT, Mashreq, Raqami) created a class of new institutions desperate for exactly two things we produce: granular deposits and thin-file underwriting data.
- **Raast**, the SBP instant-payment rail, made P2P transfers free — the cost barrier to digital committees quietly died in 2021.

**Layer 4 — the timing (why now).**
- 2025's easing cycle (policy 13%→11%) compresses bank spreads → banks hunt harder for *cheap deposits* (our supply) and *fee income* (our partnership lines).
- Smartphone penetration (~60% and climbing) + Raast + the Oraan **NBFC** precedent (SECP/LRD/107/OFSPL/2023) = behaviour, rails, and regulatory path all proven simultaneously. The category will consolidate around whoever adds **yield + identity + honesty** first.

## 2. MARKET SIZE — the honest funnel (no billion-dollar-slide games)

- **TAM** — the committee economy itself: ~US$5B/yr of rotating flow across tens of millions of adults. (Throughput, not balances — we never claim "5% of $5B".)
- **SAM** — digitally-organisable committee participants over ~5 years: **15–25M adults** (the smartphone-reachable slice of the 41%, plus host-assisted members reached through *someone else's* phone).
- **SOM** — near-term: host-led thousands of circles; the monetizable metric is **balances under management** (deposits + vault), not signups. 250k members ≈ **Rs 11B** balances; 1M ≈ Rs 45B; 2M ≈ Rs 100B.
- Anchor for credibility: the entire MFB sector holds Rs 733B of deposits. **At 1M members we'd carry ~6% of the sector's deposit equivalent; a single MFB partner absorbing our Rs 11–45B would grow its deposit base 15–50%.** That's why the partner conversation is easy: we're material to an MFB at a scale that is a rounding error to HBL.

## 4. COMPETITORS — every angle, honestly

- **The paper committee (the real competitor, ~99% share).** Free, culturally native, zero friction. Beats us on price forever. Loses on: 0% default recovery, 0% yield, 0 credit history. Our counter is *never* "stop trusting your circle" — it's "keep your circle, add a spine." Digitising *existing* circles (host brings her people) rather than matching strangers is the whole wedge.
- **Oraan (the direct pioneer).** First women-led fintech seed (~$3.31M), 10k+ savers, 84% women, 170+ cities, and — decisively — the **SECP NBFC licence**, proving the regulatory path. Model: flat service fee, backend-matched circles of strangers, no yield layer, no credit passport. Our differences: profit *engines* (their flat fee makes yield cannibalistic to add — innovator's dilemma), existing-circle digitisation (social collateral preserved), Shariah-labelled architecture, receivables/data products. Respect them publicly; their existence de-risks our category in every investor's eyes.
- **Wallets (JazzCash/easypaisa, 40M+ users).** Distribution giants; either could ship a "committee feature." Why we survive it: wallets move money, they don't organise groups, enforce discipline, or accrue portable reputation; their committee-like pilots have been shallow features, not underwriting machines; and the SimSim lesson (free + first ≠ win) cuts in our favour — *community trust is the distribution*, and that lives with hosts, not telcos. Realistic posture: wallets are our **payment rails** today and potential white-label licensees tomorrow.
- **MFBs and digital banks.** They own licences, deposits and field forces — but acquiring a *saver* costs them branches; our host acquires her for tea. They are buyers of our deposits/data/receivables, which is why we classify them as partners; the honest competitive risk is a digital bank *building* committee rails in-house — our defence is speed, the earned data moat, and being cheaper to partner with than to copy properly (the assurance layer — 283 automated checks, paisa-exact ledger, simulations — is the slow part to replicate).
- **Committee/BC apps (informal tier).** Various small register-keeper apps exist (spreadsheets with UI). None run yield engines, graduated collateral, credit files, or receivables exports. They validate demand at the low end; none are structurally comparable.
- **Alternative savings homes** (National Savings ~12–13%, bank term deposits ~11%, prize bonds, gold). These compete for the *vault* rupee, not the committee itself — and our vault simply distributes several of them, taking a trail rather than fighting them.

## 5. THE PRODUCT'S POTENTIAL FOR BANKS (the institutional prize)

What a bank actually buys from us, in its own vocabulary:
- **Granular, sticky, CASA-like deposits.** Thousands of small savers with habitual monthly behaviour — the direct cure for **deposit concentration** (MFBs have historically seen top-5 depositors at ~1/3 of deposits). Deposit-hungry institutions pay 1–2% channel commissions for exactly this.
- **A new asset class: committee receivables.** Self-amortizing, short-dated, behaviourally-collateralized inflow streams, delivered with schedules, per-payer reliability scores and collateral data (the receivables pack). For a bank, that's **receivables financing** and eventually **securitized paper** — yield assets uncorrelated with their corporate book.
- **Thin-file underwriting data at national scale.** eCIB covers ~12M borrowers; **100M+ adults are credit-invisible**. Every completed cycle mints verified repayment history the lending industry cannot buy anywhere else — as eCIB submissions (consent-based) and commercial scoring feeds.
- **Bank-side arithmetic** (the pitch): Rs 1B of committee balances ≈ ~Rs 35M/yr net spread to the bank *after* paying our channel fee, plus financing income on receivables, plus cross-sell into a verified-behaviour customer base. At 250k members (Rs 11B) an MFB partner nets ~Rs 380M+/yr — **board-level material for any MFB**, invisible to a Tier-1: we pitch the tier where we move the needle.

## 6. THE EVIDENCE — the 2025 simulation (what we can prove today)

Since no real committee-ledger dataset exists anywhere (informality is the point), we built the strongest honest substitute: an **agent-based simulation** — 120,000 synthetic members in 10,441 committees, each person with an income (HIES/LFS-calibrated bands), a discipline level, a savings buffer, and life events — living **calendar year 2025 month by month with the real year in it**: Ramadan (March), Eid-ul-Fitr (Mar 31), Eid-ul-Adha and qurbani costs (Jun 7), school-fee months (Jan/Aug/Sep), the **July–August monsoon floods** (rural-weighted damage), wedding season — including ~9% of households *hosting* a shaadi costing 4–10 months of income — medical emergencies, funerals, job losses, remittance months, and the **actual SBP rate path** (12% Jan → 11% May) with monthly CPI.

The design choice that makes it evidence: **the same 120,000 people live the same 2025 twice** — once as paper committees, once inside Halqa — and every random event (that flood, that hospital bill) hits the same person identically in both runs. So every difference in outcomes is *caused by the protection stack*, never by luck. The whole thing is deterministic: re-run it, get the same numbers to the last digit.

**Results (paper → Halqa, 2025):**

| Metric | Paper | Halqa |
|---|---|---|
| Post-payout default rate | 2.48% | **0.99%** (2.5× fewer) |
| Recovery on defaulted money | 0% | **75.3%** |
| On-time payments | 91.5% | 94.0% |
| Unresolved missed payments | 1.23% | 0.24% |
| Wedding funds completed | 84.0% | **86.1%** |
| Worst segment (over-committed) default | 10.4% | **0.94%** (11× better) |
| Member yield created (net) | — | **~Rs 69.7M** across the cohort at real 2025 rates |

The month-by-month pattern is the credibility check: lateness peaks at **Eid-ul-Adha** (qurbani), defaults peak in the **flood months**, January is calm — the model bleeds where Pakistan actually bleeds. And the caveat we volunteer before anyone asks: *it's a calibrated model, not a backtest* — the assumptions are listed with sources, the strongest claims (recovery) are near-arithmetic on collateral sizes rather than behavioural guesses, and the pilot's purpose is to replace the model with our own ledger.

## 8. DRAWBACKS & RISKS — and how each is met

- **Licence timing (the biggest).** Custody revenue waits on SECP. *Mitigation:* record-only operation is legal today and builds the data asset regardless; Oraan's licence proves the path; the raise is sized for the bridge; pre-licence fee lines keep the lights on.
- **Trust/adoption cold-start.** Savers won't trust an app with their committee. *Mitigation:* they don't have to — record-only means zero custody trust required on day one; host-led onboarding rides existing trust; Urdu/RTL and assisted flows meet users where they are.
- **Wallet/big-tech competition.** *Mitigation:* community trust ≠ distribution (SimSim); our moat is the assurance layer + earned reputation data + host network, none of which ship in a feature sprint; white-label converts would-be competitors into customers.
- **Rate compression.** SBP easing shrinks yield-linked lines. *Mitigation:* the v5 mix is majority rate-independent (service fees, receivables fees, data); safety value (not losing your pot) survives any rate regime.
- **Default-risk perception.** "Committees fail." *Mitigation:* the eleven-layer stack, the simulation's causal evidence, and post-licence teeth (balance checks, direct debit, s.489-F instruments, eCIB reporting).
- **Shariah scrutiny.** *Mitigation:* structural honesty — halal engines are architecturally halal, the conventional lever is labelled NOT-reviewed on every surface, penalties go to charity; the drawn line is the brand. Formal board certification is a named milestone.
- **Key-person risk.** Solo founder. *Mitigation:* the raise wraps compliance and growth leads around a finished, 283-check-verified system; the artifact de-risks the person.
- **Fraud/AML at custody.** *Mitigation:* compliance-as-code (ghost protocol 06): monitoring rules in versioned code, 4-eyes case closure, goAML-ready reporting — presented to SECP as the application, not retrofitted after it.

## 9. THE ONE-PARAGRAPH VERDICT

Pakistan already runs a US$5B/yr savings system with no yield, no protection and no memory, operated disproportionately by the women formal finance serves worst, in a country holding one of the world's largest unbanked populations — while its banks starve for granular deposits, its new digital banks starve for underwriting data, and its regulator pushes the whole system toward Islamic structures. One platform sitting at the junction — organising the circles, recording the truth, protecting the payouts, and selling the deposits, receivables and credit histories to the institutions that need them — monetises every side of that mismatch without taxing the saver. That junction is currently unoccupied. That is the company.

## GLOSSARY (noob-proof)

- **Findex** — the World Bank's global survey of who has financial accounts; the standard source on the unbanked.
- **Unbanked / underbanked** — no account at all / an account but little real use.
- **Financial inclusion** — bringing excluded people into formal finance; the policy goal our data serves.
- **PMN / MicroWatch** — Pakistan Microfinance Network and its quarterly sector datasheet.
- **MFB** — microfinance bank (deposit-taking, SBP-licensed): U Bank, Khushhali, Mobilink etc. Our natural partner tier.
- **NBFC** — non-bank financial company (SECP-licensed); the licence class that lets a fintech hold/manage client money. Oraan holds one.
- **EMI / digital bank** — e-money wallet licence / the new SBP full-digital-bank licences (Easypaisa Bank, Hugo, KT, Mashreq, Raqami).
- **Raast** — SBP's free instant payment rail; why committee transfers cost nothing.
- **eCIB** — the State Bank's credit bureau; ~12M borrowers on file vs 100M+ credit-invisible adults.
- **Thin-file / credit-invisible** — no formal borrowing history; unscoreable by lenders today.
- **CASA** — current & savings account money: cheap, stable deposits banks prize; committee balances behave like it.
- **Deposit concentration** — reliance on few big depositors; a chronic MFB weakness our granular deposits cure.
- **NIM / spread** — a bank's margin between asset yields and deposit costs (~4–5% gross here currently).
- **TAM / SAM / SOM** — total, serviceable, and obtainable market: the honest sizing funnel.
- **Receivables / receivables financing / securitization** — money due to arrive / lending against it / bundling many such streams into tradeable paper (**sukuk** in Islamic form).
- **Asset class** — a distinct category institutions invest in; committee receivables becoming one is the long-game.
- **Agent-based simulation** — modelling thousands of individual decision-makers and measuring what emerges; our evidence engine.
- **Dual-arm / paired design** — same population, two worlds, identical events → differences are *caused*, not lucky.
- **Calibrated model vs backtest** — assumptions tuned to public data vs testing on real history (impossible here; the pilot's job).
- **s.489-F** — Pakistan Penal Code provision making a bounced cheque a criminal offence; post-licence enforcement teeth.
- **Social collateral** — community pressure as security; the committee's engine, preserved by digitising *existing* circles.
- **Innovator's dilemma** — why an incumbent (flat-fee Oraan; spread-earning banks) struggles to copy a model that cannibalises its own revenue.
- **FSC riba directive** — the Federal Shariat Court ruling pushing Pakistan's banking system to eliminate interest (~2027 horizon); tailwind for halal-native products.
