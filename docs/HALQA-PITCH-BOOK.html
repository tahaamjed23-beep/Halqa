The problem

Half the country runs a savings machine with three fatal flaws

1. Fragile

One member collects the pot and vanishes, and the group recovers 0%. Karachi, 2022: the Sidra Humaid case. Rs 420M lost across 117 WhatsApp groups that kept no written records at all (Dawn, Express Tribune, Geo).

2. Unrewarding

Money sits idle between pay-in and payout, every round, in every circle, earning nothing while double-digit inflation eats it. A paper committee is melting ice.

3. Invisible

Twelve years of flawless payments builds zero credit history. All that discipline evaporates the day the circle ends.

The committee is not broken. It lacks infrastructure. Every failure traces back to a missing layer: enforcement without custody, yield on the idle days, or a record a lender can read.

The product

The same loop people trust, with a spine

Rule one: we record, we never hold. Members pay each other directly over Raast, wallets or cash, and record the reference. Halqa enforces schedules, collateral, scores and consequences on an integer-paisa, double-entry ledger. There is no pot to steal, so the Sidra class of fraud is structurally impossible here, and no custody licence is needed to operate today.

What paper can never add

  • Protection. Deposits sized to each member's exposure (the host sets 30-90%), payout holdbacks, reliability scores, capped stakes for newcomers, and an optional member-funded Safety Fund.
  • Profit. Idle days and collateral earn on disclosed Islamic instruments, automatically.
  • Reputation. A signed, independently verifiable Credit Passport for each member, and a bank-grade receivables statement for each circle.
  • Honesty. Halal engines carry a halal label. The one conventional lever is marked "not Shariah-reviewed" on every screen it touches. The word "guaranteed" appears nowhere in the product.

Built and verified today

  • Full committee lifecycle across five configurable tiers, with live circles from Rs 50k to Rs 500k pots
  • Personal halal savings vault with four yield tiers, plus auto-cover self-insurance
  • A turn marketplace: free swaps everywhere, premium trades in the conventional tiers only
  • One-tap Urdu with full right-to-left layout; installs to the phone like a native app
  • An in-app assistant with a guided tutorial and an honest FAQ of about 120 topics
  • 25 unit and 283 end-to-end automated checks, including simulated attacks, run clean twice in a row
Stage 1 needs no licence: it earns modestly, builds trust, and accrues data. Stage 2 (the SECP NBFC licence plus a bank partner) switches the same ledger over to real custody. That switch is wiring work, since every design decision is already made.

How members earn

Two honest sources: profit that is created, and patience that is priced

Created (halal-labelled)

  • Float sweep. Every idle day between pay-in and payout earns at the Islamic money-market rate, computed per payment, per day.
  • Earning collateral. Security deposits sit in Mudarabah placements for the whole cycle. The protection pays its own participants, and raising coverage makes a circle safer and richer at the same time.
  • Patience tilt. Cycle profit splits by capital-days with a position weight rising from 1.0 to 2.0, so the late turns that fund the circle finally earn a term premium.
  • Behaviour rewards. Consistent early payers carry a 1.25x weight, funded by the extra float their own early money creates. A fully punctual circle earns yield step-ups for everyone.

Moved (labelled not Shariah-reviewed)

  • Early-turn fee. Capped at 10%, declining to zero by the last turn, redistributed entirely to the waiting members. Halqa keeps none of it.

Risk engine

Eleven coded safeguards against the one moment that kills committees

The dangerous moment is the post-payout default: collect the pot, then stop paying. The safeguards fire in this order:

  • Reliability score gates (550 to join, 700 to host), updated on every payment event
  • A first-circle cap of Rs 25,000 per installment until one clean completed cycle
  • Credit-weighted ordering, which places trusted members in the high-exposure early turns
  • Graduated deposits: coverage% of remaining dues, host-set between 30 and 90%
  • A 15% payout holdback, released as the recipient keeps paying cleanly
  • Reminders, peer nudges, and a calibrated grace window whose length is never disclosed
  • Flat progressive penalties that go to charity or to the never-late members, never to Halqa
  • Vault auto-cover, where a member's own savings settle her own missed installment
  • An optional Safety Fund guaranteeing payouts up to the fund's recorded balance
  • Ban plus collateral forfeiture, with a priced rehabilitation path back
  • After licensing: bank balance checks, direct debit mandates, and eCIB reporting

Measured in a full-year simulation

120,000 agents in 10,441 committees lived calendar 2025 month by month: Ramadan, both Eids, school-fee season, the monsoon floods, wedding season, and the actual SBP rate path. The same population lived the year twice, once as paper committees and once inside Halqa, with every random event identical across both runs, so the differences below are attributable to the safeguards. The institutional prize

What a bank actually buys from us

  • Granular, sticky deposits. Thousands of small, habitual savers behave like the current-and-savings money banks fight over, and they cure the deposit-concentration problem. Rs 1B of balances leaves the bank roughly Rs 35M a year in net spread after paying our commission.
  • A new asset class: committee receivables. Short-dated, self-amortizing inflow streams backed by behavioural collateral, delivered with schedules, per-payer reliability scores and collateral data. Financeable today; securitizable at scale.
  • Underwriting data for the credit-invisible. eCIB covers about 12M borrowers, while more than 100M adults have no file at all. Every completed cycle mints verified repayment history that cannot be bought anywhere else. Esusu built a billion-dollar company in the US on this layer alone.
The same Rs 45B of balances is 0.15% of the banking sector, about 6% of the entire MFB sector, and over 60% of a single mid-size MFB. We pitch the tier where we change the institution's year.

Go-to-market

The organiser auntie is the distribution

  • The keystone. Pakistan has roughly 2-3M active committee organisers. One demonstration over chai brings in 6 to 20 pre-trusted members with their circle intact. Acquisition cost is a relationship rather than a media budget, and the host never pays.
  • Referral discipline. Rs 250 per referred member, paid only when that member completes a first full cycle, funded from our own revenue. Growth spending buys finished circles rather than downloads.
  • Segment order. Home-business women first (the best payment discipline in our data), then office circles, then the bazaar daily committees that no digital player serves, then professional circles with Rs 500k pots, and eventually diaspora members funding family committees through the $30B remittance corridor.
  • Urdu-first, assisted onboarding, built for households where the member often does not own the phone she uses.
